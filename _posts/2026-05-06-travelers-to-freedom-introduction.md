This post introduces the digital history project I have been working on this semester: mapping the routes of travelers escaping slavery in 1855 and 1856 by using Sydney Howard Gay’s Underground Railroad records to create a geography-sensitive database. The resulting spatial data depicts the journeys of freedom seekers. It reveals how freedom seekers made their escapes, shedding light on often-overlooked participants in anti-slavery action. Geodata and primary accounts of escapes illustrate freedom seekers’ experiences of travel, space, and motion.

<h1>Project origins</h1>

<p>After I moved to Rochester in 2024, I spent a summer as a seasonal park ranger at Harriet Tubman National Historical Park in Auburn, NY. Harriet Tubman is the most recognizable Underground Railroad figure for most people. She led groups of freedom seekers through Auburn, where she eventually settled her family, and to Rochester. In Rochester, Tubman brought freedom seekers to stay with abolitionist writer and speaker Frederick Douglass, who had made his own journey to freedom as a young man.</p>

<figure>
    <img src="/images/travelers-to-freedom/1868_Powelson-Benjamin_Portrait-of-Harriet-Tubman.jpg" width="274px" height="435px">
    <figcaption>Harriet Tubman, photographed ca. 1868 by Benjamin Powelson.<sup><a href="#fn1" id="r1">[1]</a></sup></figcaption>
</figure>

<p>Rochester was likely an obvious safe haven for Tubman and the freedom seekers she guided. Tubman and Douglass were from neighboring counties in Maryland, and might have been familiar with each other before they were free. They may have even been related. At the very least, they formed an effective partnership when it came to the Underground Railroad. Rochester’s proximity to the Canadian border made Douglass’s house the final stop in the US for most freedom seekers; Tubman delivered them straight to his door.<sup><a href="#fn2" id="r2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>My time as a ranger made me more curious about the freedom seekers who traveled without Tubman’s guidance. As I made the daily commute from my apartment in Rochester (a five-minute drive from the location where Frederick Douglass’s house once stood) to the Harriet Tubman National Historical Park (a five-minute drive from several of the former Underground Railroad sites in Auburn), I wondered how lone freedom seekers made it to Rochester. Where did they travel from? Who directed them to Douglass’s house, and how did they get there? What obstacles did they face?</p>

<h1>What stories do we tell about the Underground Railroad?</h1>

<p>In truth, Underground Railroad history tends to focus on the places where freedom seekers stopped rather than their experiences of travel. Harriet Tubman is probably the most prominent exception to this tendency. Because her skill as a naturalist and navigator is so famous, we tend to picture her outside, forging through Chesapeake wetlands.</p>

<figure>
    <img src="/images/travelers-to-freedom/2019_Rosato-Michael_Harriet-Tubman-Mural.jpg">
    <figcaption>Harriet Tubman mural by Michael Rosato, 2019.<sup><a href="#fn4" id="r4">[4]</a></sup></figcaption>
<figure>

<p>Apart from Tubman’s example, however, the most well-known part of the Underground Railroad are the places we now call “stations”—the homes or businesses of people who hosted freedom seekers. This approach makes sense for individual sites; we can expect interpretation of a house museum, for example, to focus on the homeowners and their use of the building for anti-slavery purposes. For example, the map below shows station sites included on the National Park Service Network to Freedom list and sites with historical markers related to abolition and the Underground Railroad. These are public history commemorations; many of them center local history.</p>

</figure>

<p>The station-oriented approach has some drawbacks when we consider the Underground Railroad as a whole, however. Focusing on an individual location, or even many individual locations within a network, can cause us to lose perspective.</p>

<p>Sometimes, this occurs through sentimentalization or mythologization. At its least factual, Underground Railroad history can become a type of folklore. There are certainly far more buildings and people rumored to be involved with the Underground Railroad than actually were involved. Everyone wants their ancestor or local house museum to have a connection to something significant and heroic; Underground Railroad participation is an easy suggestion to make, and all but impossible to definitively disprove.</p> 

<p>Mythologizing the Underground Railroad does a disservice to abolitionists and Underground Railroad helpers by making it seem as though their belief was widespread. It was not. Abolitionism was radical, and people willing to consistently and publicly support it were very few.<sup><a href="#fn6" id="r6">[6]</a></sup> We in the twenty-first century are in the privileged position of knowing that the abolitionists won—slavery was abolished. The people who fought for its abolition might have hoped that it would happen in their lifetimes, but they must have experienced much of the abolition campaign as an interminable uphill battle in which they were vastly outnumbered.</p>

<p>Imagining the Underground Railroad as a vast and well-organized network also does a disservice to freedom seekers by portraying them as passive “passengers.” In reality, the majority of freedom seekers planned their journeys without the expectation of substantial help. Even people who knew that they might find a support system in the north still had to travel there and seek assistance. At some point, <i>every</i> freedom seeker made the decision to take the extraordinary risk of traveling to freedom. As the data in this project emphasizes, these journeys were largely self-directed. Myths about secret codes and well-organized networks can obscure the things that freedom seekers actually relied on: their own courage and resourcefulness.</p>

<p>We can see examples of Underground Railroad mythology in many places. Take a look at two of the listings from the Historical Marker Database:</p>

<p>The first marker commemorates the Carpenter’s Wheel quilt pattern and its connection to the Underground Railroad and the Hagood-Mauldin historical house museum. As much as I love recognition of textile arts in history, there are a few problems here. First of all, the idea of quilts as a form of communication with freedom seekers is almost certainly a myth; there is no evidence that anyone involved with the Underground Railroad used quilts to send secret signals.<sup><a href="#fn8" id="r8">[8]</a></sup> Also, the historical house doesn’t seem to have any connections to abolition or to the Underground Railroad (in fact, its notable residents were a Confederate colonel and his daughter, who served as the president of the South Carolina United Daughters of the Confederacy).<sup><a href="#fn9" id="r9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The second marker, in Wilmington, North Carolina, marks the point from which eight enslaved men, including twenty-five-year-old William Benjamin Gould, launched a boat on the night of September 21, 1862. Gould and his comrades rowed this vessel approximately thirty miles down the Cape Fear River, past the Confederate-held Fort Caswell, and to the Atlantic Ocean, where Union naval ships maintained a blockade. The U.S.S. <i>Cambridge</i> intercepted their boat, and Gould enlisted in the navy. During his wartime service, he kept a diary (the pictures of which are available on the <a href="https://law.stanford.edu/william-b-gould-iv/diary-of-a-contraband/">Stanford Law School’s website</a>). The historical marker commemorating Gould’s launch is, in some ways, local history; Gould and his fellow freedom seekers took advantage of Wilmington’s situation on the Cape Fear River in order to make their escape.<sup><a href="#fn10" id="r10">[10]</a></sup> But the marker also highlights Gould’s—and Wilmington’s—relationship to a much larger wartime and maritime context. It centers Gould’s actions, and those of his comrades, as people who made the choice to leave Wilmington and undertook extraordinary risk to do so.</p>

<p>Despite its strengths, however, this marker isn’t perfect either. It describes the Underground Railroad as “a secret cooperative network of clandestine routes and safe houses that aided runaway slaves in reaching northern free states or Canada,” an overstatement of its organization and geographic extent.<sup><a href="#fn11" id="r11">[11]</a></sup> Moreover, can we really apply the term “Underground Railroad” to William Benjamin Gould’s journey when he and his fellow freedom seekers planned their own escape and traveled to freedom on their own?</p>

<p>I don’t draw this comparison to disparage either historical marker, both of which are well-intentioned and serve to promote local history. My intent is to illustrate that different ways of framing the Underground Railroad serve different purposes. The goal of establishing a local connection to the Underground Railroad can eclipse the goal of accurately reflecting how it worked or of centering the experiences of freedom seekers.</p>

<p>This brings us to another problem with station-focused history: its geographic limitation. If we focus on the places where freedom seekers stopped on their way north, we emphasize the most static parts of their journey. The Underground Railroad was a system of travel. It existed because thousands of people chose to seek their freedom. Their journeys connected the people who helped them, turning disparate abolitionists into supporters of a common goal.<sup><a href="#fn12" id="r12">[12]</a></sup></p>
